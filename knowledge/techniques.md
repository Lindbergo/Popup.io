# Pop-Up Card Techniques: Complete Reference

This file is the authoritative knowledge base for all pop-up card mechanisms. Every AI-generated design must reference this file and must not invent techniques outside it.

---

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Gutter** | The center fold line of the base card — the spine |
| **Valley fold** | A fold where the paper dips down (like a valley). Shown as dashed lines on templates |
| **Mountain fold** | A fold where the paper peaks up (like a mountain). Shown as dot-dash lines on templates |
| **Score line** | A pressed crease made before folding, required for clean folds on cardstock ≥ 80 lb |
| **Pedestal** | A strip of paper glued perpendicular to a surface that holds a floating element up |
| **Tab** | A flap of paper used for gluing one piece to another |
| **Pop-up element** | Any piece of paper that rises up when the card is opened |

## Paper Specifications

### Recommended Paper Weights

| Use | Weight | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Base card | 110 lb (300 gsm) | Sturdy, holds mechanisms firmly |
| Pop-up elements | 80–90 lb (216–245 gsm) | Flexible enough to fold, strong enough to hold shape |
| Small detail pieces | 65–80 lb (176–216 gsm) | For decorative elements that don't bear load |
| Photo inserts | Any weight | Glued flat onto pop-up elements — no structural role |

### Standard Card Sizes

| Format | Flat dimensions | Folded dimensions |
|---|---|---|
| A2 (US standard) | 5.5" × 8.5" | 4.25" × 5.5" |
| A6 | 4.5" × 6.25" | 3.125" × 4.5" |
| Square (small) | 6" × 6" | 3" × 6" |
| Square (large) | 8" × 8" | 4" × 8" |

### Tools Required (all designs)

- Scoring tool or bone folder
- Ruler (metal edge preferred)
- Craft knife or X-acto knife
- Cutting mat
- PVA glue or double-sided tape
- Pencil

## Universal Design Rules

These rules apply to ALL techniques. A design that violates these rules will not function correctly.

1. **Nothing can extend beyond the card edges when closed.** Every pop-up element must fold flat within the card footprint.
2. **All score/fold lines must be straight.** No curved folds — curved shapes are achieved by cutting, not folding.
3. **Cuts run from valley to mountain to valley** (they always cross a fold line).
4. **Fold lines alternate** between mountain and valley — never two mountains or two valleys adjacent.
5. **Test before decorating.** Always build the mechanism, test it opens and closes 3+ times, then add decoration.
6. **Glue placement is critical.** Glue attaches to the base at a precise distance from the gutter — too close or far and the mechanism binds or collapses.

## Mechanism Catalogue

---

### 1. V-Fold

**What it is:** The most fundamental pop-up mechanism. A flat piece of paper with a fold at its center is glued to the base card at an angle so that opening the card forces the piece upright.

**Best for:** Trees, flames, hearts, mountains, text letters, simple shapes that rise vertically.

**Difficulty:** Beginner

**How it works structurally:**
- The base card is folded in half (the gutter).
- A separate piece is folded in half at its center (the V-fold crease).
- The two halves of the piece are glued to the two halves of the base card symmetrically, with the fold crease of the piece sitting directly on — or parallel to — the gutter.
- When the card opens 90°, the piece is forced upright. When closed, it folds flat.

**Geometric constraints:**
- The V-fold crease **must** sit on the gutter or at a line parallel to the gutter.
- The angle of the glue attachment controls the direction the piece leans when open (left, right, or straight up).
- Both halves of the piece must be **mirror images** if the piece is to stand straight.
- Height of the element when open = half the flat width of the piece.

**Template markup:**
- Draw the flat piece, marking its center fold line.
- Mark the glue tabs (hatched areas) on each half.
- Mark the gutter line on the base card where each half attaches.
- Note: the glue line on each half must be equidistant from the V-fold crease.

**Step-by-step (generic):**
1. Cut the pop-up shape from cardstock (80 lb recommended).
2. Score and fold the piece in half at its center — this becomes the V-fold crease.
3. Score and fold the base card in half at the gutter.
4. Open the base card flat. Apply glue to the underside of the left half-tab of the piece.
5. Align the piece crease with the gutter, press the left tab onto the left half of the base.
6. Fold the card closed — the right half of the piece will self-position against the right half of the base. Press down to bond.
7. Allow to dry fully before testing.

### 2. Box Fold (Parallel Fold Pop-Up)

**What it is:** Two parallel cuts made from the gutter, with a connecting fold between them, create a rectangular box or platform that stands up when the card opens.

**Best for:** Tables, stages, pedestals, buildings, shelves, platforms — any flat horizontal surface that needs to pop up.

**Difficulty:** Beginner–Intermediate

**How it works structurally:**
- Two equal-length cuts are made parallel to each other, starting from the gutter edge of a folded card.
- A new fold connects the inner ends of the two cuts, parallel to the gutter.
- This creates a rectangular flap that, when reverse-folded (against the direction of the main card fold), pops out to form a box/platform perpendicular to the card surface.

**Geometric constraints:**
- Both cuts must be **equal length** — call this length `d`.
- The distance between the two cuts = the width of the box `w`.
- The connecting fold runs parallel to the gutter at distance `d` from the gutter.
- The box height when open = `d`.
- The box top surface = `w × d`.
- Minimum clearance from the card edge: `d + 5mm`.

**Cutting and folding procedure:**
1. Fold card in half at gutter.
2. Mark two parallel lines on the folded edge, each of length `d`, separated by distance `w`.
3. Cut along these two lines (cut through both layers of the folded card).
4. Mark and score the connecting fold at distance `d` from the gutter — parallel to gutter.
5. Open the card. Reverse-fold the rectangular flap (push it toward you so it folds opposite to the main card fold).
6. Crease the new fold firmly. Close the card — the box should flatten. Open — it should pop up.

**Template markup:**
- Mark cut lines in red (solid bold).
- Mark the gutter fold in blue (dashed).
- Mark the connecting fold in green (dashed — it's a valley fold on the box face).
- Label `w` (box width) and `d` (box depth/height).

**Common scene uses:**
- **Table:** Box fold creates the tabletop. Legs are decorative strips glued to the front face of the box.
- **Stage/Platform:** Box fold creates the stage floor. Curtains are V-fold elements glued to the stage.
- **Building:** Multiple box folds at different depths create a cityscape.

### 3. Floating Layer (Three-Pedestal Lift)

**What it is:** A flat panel is held above the base card by three equal-height pedestals, creating the illusion of a floating layer. The panel can carry images, text, or further mechanisms.

**Best for:** Layered scenes with depth, portrait panels, sky layers behind foreground elements, image frames.

**Difficulty:** Intermediate

**How it works structurally:**
- Three rectangular pedestals (strips of cardstock folded in half lengthwise) are glued perpendicular to the base card.
- All three must be **exactly the same height** — any variation causes the layer to tilt.
- The floating panel is glued across the tops of all three pedestals.
- Two pedestals are at the back, one is at the front (triangular support = no wobble).

**Geometric constraints:**
- All 3 pedestals: same height `h`, width ≥ 5mm each.
- Pedestal position: two at rear edge of panel, one centered at front edge.
- Floating panel must fit within card boundaries when closed — the card needs to be taller by at least `h + panel thickness`.
- Combined structure (base → pedestal → panel) must fold flat: pedestals fold inward.

**Template markup:**
- Base card showing three pedestal glue positions (marked with X patterns).
- Pedestal strip dimensions (flat: width = `h × 2`, height = pedestal strip width).
- Floating panel with glue tabs at three attach points.

### 4. Angle (Off-Center) V-Fold

**What it is:** A V-fold where the fold crease is not parallel to the gutter but set at an angle, causing the pop-up element to lean or pivot when the card opens.

**Best for:** Characters turning to face the viewer, directional elements (a pointing finger, a leaning tree), angled architecture.

**Difficulty:** Intermediate

**How it works structurally:**
- Same as V-fold, but the attachment angle of each tab is asymmetric.
- The fold crease of the piece intersects the gutter at a non-right angle.
- The angle of lean when open = the angle difference from perpendicular.

**Geometric constraints:**
- The two glue lines on the base (one per half) must be mirror images across the gutter.
- The tighter the angle to the gutter, the more the piece leans rather than stands upright.
- Minimum angle from gutter: 15° (below this, the piece lies nearly flat and the mechanism is weak).

### 5. Moving Arm (Pivot Mechanism)

**What it is:** A piece is attached to the card at one point (the pivot) and to a pull-tab at another. Moving the tab causes the piece to rotate around the pivot.

**Best for:** Waving hands, opening doors, nodding heads, clocks with moving hands.

**Difficulty:** Intermediate–Advanced

**How it works structurally:**
- A pivot brad or glued pivot point anchors one end of the arm to the base card.
- A pull-strip runs through a slit in the card and attaches to the other end of the arm.
- Pulling the strip moves the arm; releasing lets it return (no spring — the user controls position).

**Geometric constraints:**
- Pivot point to arm tip = arm length `L`.
- Arc of motion = limited by card boundaries (arm tip must stay within card at all positions).
- Pull-strip width: 5–8mm, length = arc of motion + handle length (min 20mm handle).
- Slit for pull-strip must be wider than strip by 1mm on each side.

---

### 6. Sliding Motion (Pull-Tab)

**What it is:** An element is attached to a strip that slides through a slit, so pulling the strip causes the element to move linearly across the card.

**Best for:** Sliding doors, characters entering/exiting from the side, text reveals, drawers opening.

**Difficulty:** Intermediate

**How it works structurally:**
- A slit is cut in the base card parallel to the direction of motion.
- A strip passes through the slit; the element is glued on top of the strip on the card face.
- A sleeve (folded strip) on the underside guides the strip and prevents it from pulling free.
- Pulling the exposed tab moves the element.

**Geometric constraints:**
- Slit length = range of motion + element width.
- Strip length = slit length + handle (≥ 20mm) + sleeve engagement (≥ 15mm).
- Sleeve height must match strip thickness (just loose enough to slide freely).
- Element must not extend beyond card edge at any position in the range of motion.

---

### 7. Rotating Disc

**What it is:** A circular disc mounted on a central pivot can spin on the card surface, revealing different images or creating a spinning animation effect.

**Best for:** Clock faces, wheels, kaleidoscope effects, "spin the wheel" interactives.

**Difficulty:** Advanced

**How it works structurally:**
- A disc is attached to the base card at its exact center point with a brad.
- A parallelogram strip on the long side of the disc base keeps the disc upright as the card opens/closes.
- The disc sits in a window cut in the card front.

**Geometric constraints:**
- Disc diameter must be ≤ card height − 20mm (clearance all around).
- Brad must be at exact center of disc.
- The keeper strip (parallelogram) width = disc circumference × (angle of card open ÷ 360°).

---

### 8. Tunnel/Layered Depth

**What it is:** Multiple flat panels are stacked at increasing distances from the gutter, each with a cutout, creating a tunnel perspective effect.

**Best for:** Forest scenes, hallways, theater stages, depth illusions.

**Difficulty:** Intermediate–Advanced

**How it works structurally:**
- Layer 1 (front): Closest to viewer, largest cutout, attached closest to gutter edge.
- Layer 2, 3, etc.: Progressively further from front, smaller cutouts, each attached further from gutter.
- Final layer (back): Solid background image.
- Each layer is attached to the base card at its bottom edge with a fold tab.

**Geometric constraints:**
- Spacing between layers: 10–20mm (enough to see each layer clearly).
- Each layer's cutout must be smaller than the one in front of it.
- Total stack depth must fit within card height when closed.
- Each layer folds down flat independently.

---

### 9. Pop-Up Text / Letters

**What it is:** Each letter of a word is constructed as a V-fold element, creating 3D text that pops up when the card opens.

**Best for:** "Happy Birthday", "I Love You", names, single words as focal point.

**Difficulty:** Beginner–Intermediate

**How it works structurally:**
- Each letter is designed as a symmetric shape that can be folded in half vertically.
- Letters are individual V-fold pieces spaced along the gutter.
- Simple block letters work best — avoid thin strokes (they tear).

**Geometric constraints:**
- Letter height: 20–50mm (max height = half the card width).
- Minimum stroke width in any letter: 5mm.
- Spacing between letters: 5–10mm.
- Total word width must not exceed card length − 20mm.

## Scene Composition Guide

When a user describes a scene (people, objects, environments), map elements to mechanisms as follows:

| Scene element | Recommended mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Table, platform, stage | Box Fold | Adjust `w` and `d` for table proportions |
| Chair (side view) | V-Fold | Use an L-shaped piece for seat + back |
| Chair (front-on) | V-Fold | Symmetric piece, seat visible from front |
| Person sitting | V-Fold element on top of chair piece | Photo of person glued to a card panel, panel glued to chair V-fold |
| Person standing | V-Fold | Full-body silhouette, folded at waist |
| Tree / plant | V-Fold | Symmetric tree shape |
| Building | Box Fold (stacked) | Multiple boxes at different depths |
| Sky / background | Floating Layer | Full-width panel at back |
| Text message | Pop-Up Text V-Folds | Or flat text on floating layer |
| Door that opens | Moving Arm | Pivot at door edge |
| Animal | V-Fold or Angle V-Fold | Adjust based on pose |

### Example: "Table with two people sitting"

This is a multi-mechanism design:

1. **Table** → Box Fold. Dimensions: `w` = 60mm, `d` = 30mm (tabletop 60×30mm, table height 30mm when open). Position: centered on gutter.
2. **Chair Left** → V-Fold piece glued to the left half of the base card, in front of the table. Chair piece: seat (30×20mm) + back (30×25mm), folded at the seat-back joint.
3. **Chair Right** → Mirror-image V-Fold of Chair Left, glued to the right half of the base card.
4. **Person Left** → Photo/illustration panel (25×40mm) glued flat to the chair back of Chair Left.
5. **Person Right** → Photo/illustration panel (25×40mm) glued flat to the chair back of Chair Right.
6. **Table surface** → Optional decorative elements (plates, glasses) glued flat to the box-fold tabletop.
7. **Background** → Floating layer or printed background glued to card interior back.

**Assembly order (critical):** Background first → Table box fold → Left chair → Right chair → Photo panels last.

## Template Color Convention

All printed templates use this color system consistently:

| Color | Line type | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Red | Solid bold | Cut line — cut all the way through |
| Blue | Dashed | Valley fold — fold toward you |
| Green | Dash-dot | Mountain fold — fold away from you |
| Gray | Solid thin | Score line (press, don't cut) |
| Yellow fill | Hatched area | Glue zone — apply adhesive here |

## Failure Modes & Fixes

| Problem | Likely cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Element doesn't pop up fully | Glue tabs too short / too far from gutter | Move glue attachment point closer to gutter |
| Card won't close flat | Element too tall, or extends past card edge | Reduce height `d` or trim element |
| Fold is lumpy/irregular | Cardstock not scored before folding | Always score first, then fold |
| Element tears at fold | Paper too thin for mechanism size | Use heavier paper or reduce element size |
| Box fold collapses | Connecting fold not reverse-folded firmly | Crease the reverse fold harder; ensure it's opposite direction to main fold |
| Pedestals make layer tilt | Pedestals not identical height | Re-cut all three to exactly equal height |
| Pull-tab jams | Slit too narrow | Widen slit by 0.5mm on each side |
